Giornale I

Fineas Jackson

May 25, 2023 • 2 min read

We jump out of the car the second my mom flicks the gear shifter to park. We’re late; traffic was bumper to bumper for the last forty minutes of the ride. Inside the airport I find total chaos. Check in kiosk’s have lines four families deep, and the baggage drop off seems to span endlessly, like Tartarus.

Qavalina manages to print her checked bag ticket. The instant it has left the machine, she rushes off to the bag drop-off. I look around. The fear, impatience, and anger in the air is palpable. I understand then that it’s every person for themselves. There are no friends here.

My machine sends me on a different path than Qavalina. I receive a ticket that beckons me to the left, to the full-service bag line. The ticket says I need to speak with an agent. I don’t know why, and I want to try checking in again, but the hot air on my neck from the man standing behind me, advises me to do otherwise.

The full-service bag line is even more infinite than the bag drop-off. At this point, the pee I’ve been holding since the start of the car ride is screaming to be released. By the time I’ve made it around the third loop of the cordoned off area, each step is a struggle. I can’t remember ever needing to pee this badly. I calm myself by thinking of the strength I’m building. The more I endure, the stronger I’ll become.

This thought comes to my mind again, when the attendant tells me that my flight is no longer taking checked bags. “Sorry, 6 PM is the cut-off”, she says. I stop for a second. Then I realize, “Wait isn’t it 6:02?”. She looks at me grimly. “There’s nothing I can do.”

A thousand thoughts and fears run through my head. Through all of them one message resounds clearer, louder. “FIND THE BATHROOM.” And I’m off. Hobbling, with my massive duffel bag in my right hand, I make my way to the back of the lobby. Dismay ripples through every particle of my being when I arrive at the bathroom. There’s a line.

I have never needed to pee this bad before. I probably missed my plane. Can I even do this? Should I just let it all go? It’s a breaking point, a do or die moment. What path should I choose? I take a deep breath, and then it all makes sense.

This is another challenge. Another chance to test myself. I always have performed well under pressure. What will I do here? Collapse in a puddle of piss and sorrow? I think not. This is an opportunity to see how much I can take. And this is not where I will be broken.

As I mentally recount the story 30,000 feet in the air, I smile softly to myself. The madness of that day only continued. An hour and a half wait in line to change my flight, only to be told I had to rebook through my travel agency. A sleepless night, messaging back and forth with the trip supervisor. My mom, both yelling and crying. Guilt that she wasn’t fast enough, and anger that I hadn’t done enough.

Yet, here I was, on the path to Rome. And I was very much still in one piece.
